# On System Overhead

Yes, this system has real weight to it.

YAML frontmatter on every note, MOC parents, Children lists, status tags, bidirectional links, weekly health checks, note-type rules with required fields — that's a non-trivial amount of overhead per note, and it compounds. There will be sessions where maintaining the system feels like more work than the learning itself. That's a legitimate cost, not a perception problem.

A few things will happen that are worth acknowledging upfront:

A Seedling sits untouched for six weeks because life happened. A ResearchQueue item stays open for months because it stopped feeling urgent. The health check exists precisely because none of this is self-correcting — it requires **deliberate maintenance**, and that maintenance will sometimes be skipped.

The system will also occasionally slow down note-writing in the moment. Deciding which MOC a note belongs to, whether something is a Pattern or a Concept, whether an observation warrants a note at all — these are small friction points that add up, especially early when the **rules aren't yet instinct.**

None of this is a reason to simplify the architecture. The constraints exist because looser systems fail in predictable ways — and the failure mode of an undisciplined vault (orphaned notes, meaningless links, no way back in) is worse than occasional maintenance debt. But it is worth being clear-eyed: this is a system that requires upkeep, and the upkeep is part of the commitment.

How do you mitigate it?
- Keep your notes concise, follow the templates provided, run weekly 15minute health checkups.
- **Use Dataview for adding children to MOC notes automatically. (MOST IMPORTANT)**
or/and
- Use Obsidian Copilot or other AI tools to act as a warden for your vault. Give it the principles, vault_designs notes as instructions. (Duplicate your vault first - results may be unpredictable)
